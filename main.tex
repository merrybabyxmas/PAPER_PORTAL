\begin{abstract}
%=============================================================================
Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods such as LoRA statically fix the adapter topology to a \emph{parallel} connection, fundamentally bypassing the rich pretrained weight manifold ($\Wbase$) that earlier sequential adapters successfully exploited for rapid task adaptation.
While recent SVD-based initialization methods (e.g., PiSSA) attempt to utilize $\Wbase$, they suffer from prohibitive $O(d^3)$ initialization bottlenecks and remain inherently task-agnostic.
In this paper, we introduce \portal{}, a generalized PEFT framework that unifies sequential and parallel adaptations along the training time axis.
During an initial sequential phase, \portal{} utilizes $\Wbase$ as a projection lens on the activations ($\Wbase x$) to conduct an ultra-fast, data-driven exploration of the task-intrinsic subspace.
At an optimal switching step $\Tswitch$, a lossless \emph{Task-Isomorphic Projection} seamlessly converts the learned sequential representations into an equivalent parallel adapter via a lightweight rank-$r$ double SVD.
This transformation incurs a negligible $O(dr^2)$ computational cost---orders of magnitude cheaper than full-weight SVD---while perfectly preserving the \emph{zero additional latency} property of LoRA at inference.
Standard LoRA is mathematically recovered as the special case $\Tswitch = 0$.
Extensive experiments on LLaMA-2-7B and DeBERTa-v3-base across mathematical reasoning (GSM8K, MATH), code generation (HumanEval, MBPP), conversation (MT-Bench), and seven GLUE tasks demonstrate that \portal{} consistently outperforms LoRA, PiSSA, and DoRA, achieving the best score on 6 out of 7 NLU tasks and the highest MATH accuracy.
Furthermore, we empirically reveal that $\Tswitch$ serves as a critical geometric indicator of the alignment between the target task and the pretrained manifold.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

Large language models (LLMs) with billions of parameters~\citep{brown2020gpt3, touvron2023llama2} have become the \emph{de facto} foundation for diverse natural language processing and reasoning tasks. 
However, full fine-tuning of such immense models is prohibitively expensive in terms of compute and memory. 
This bottleneck has motivated the rapid development of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods, which update only a minuscule fraction of parameters while keeping the pretrained weights frozen~\citep{lialin2023scaling}.

Among PEFT methodologies, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)~\citep{hu2022lora} has emerged as the dominant paradigm. 
LoRA injects trainable low-rank matrices $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}$ in \emph{parallel} with each frozen weight $\Wbase$, yielding the forward pass $h = \Wbase x + BAx$. 
This parallel topology is highly celebrated because the adapter can be reparameterized into $\Wbase$ after training, guaranteeing \emph{zero additional inference latency}. 
However, LoRA typically relies on random initialization for its adapter weights. Consequently, the early optimization steps operate in a subspace entirely disconnected from the rich, pretrained representations of $\Wbase$, often leading to suboptimal early trajectories and slow initial convergence.

To mitigate this, recent approaches such as PiSSA~\citep{meng2024pissa} initialize $B$ and $A$ using the principal singular vectors of $\Wbase$. 
While this SVD-based initialization significantly accelerates convergence, it introduces a severe computational bottleneck: it requires a full SVD over every massive target weight matrix, incurring a prohibitive $O(d^3)$ cost per layer. 
Furthermore, because this decomposition is performed statically before observing any data, it merely captures the raw architectural structure of $\Wbase$, remaining fundamentally \emph{task-agnostic}.

Interestingly, the earliest PEFT methods employed a \emph{sequential} topology~\citep{houlsby2019parameter}, where the adapter processes the output of the base layer: $h = \Wbase x + BA(\Wbase x)$. 
This structure possesses a profound, often overlooked mathematical advantage: it utilizes the feature space of $\Wbase$ as an implicit prior, acting as a projection lens to efficiently extract task-relevant signals. 
Ultimately, sequential adapters were largely abandoned in favor of parallel methods because their sequential nature incurs inference latency strictly proportional to the model's depth.

We challenge the prevailing assumption that the choice between a sequential and parallel topology must be mutually exclusive and static. 
In this paper, we introduce \portal{} (\textbf{P}arameter-efficient \textbf{O}ptimization via \textbf{R}ank-bounded \textbf{T}ask-isomorphic \textbf{A}dapter \textbf{L}ifting), a framework that \emph{generalizes} adapter connectivity over the training time axis. 
By treating the structural transition as a continuous variable rather than a discrete architectural choice, \portal{} synthesizes the optimization benefits of sequential probing with the inference efficiency of parallel adaptation. Our main contributions are four-fold:

\begin{itemize}
    \item[\bf C1.] \textbf{Time-Axis Generalized PEFT Framework:} We formulate a novel paradigm where the adapter topology dynamically transitions from sequential to parallel at a configurable switching point $\Tswitch \in [0, 1]$. Standard LoRA is mathematically unified within this framework as the extreme special case where $\Tswitch = 0$.
    \item[\bf C2.] \textbf{Task-Aware Manifold Probing:} We demonstrate that the initial sequential phase acts as a highly efficient, data-driven probe of the pretrained manifold. This enables the model to rapidly identify the optimal task-intrinsic subspace, achieving faster and more stable convergence than random (LoRA) or static (PiSSA) initializations.
    \item[\bf C3.] \textbf{Fast SVD Initialization:} We propose a \emph{Task-Isomorphic Projection} that seamlessly converts the learned sequential representations into an equivalent parallel adapter via a lightweight rank-$r$ double SVD. This operation incurs a negligible $O(dr^2)$ cost, completely eliminating PiSSA's $O(d^3)$ initialization bottleneck without any loss of representation power.
    \item[\bf C4.] \textbf{Zero Inference Latency:} Following the projection at $\Tswitch$, the final model continues and concludes training in a purely parallel state. It is structurally identical to standard LoRA, perfectly preserving the zero additional inference latency property.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{Parallel Low-Rank Adaptation and Optimization.}
The paradigm of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) has been heavily dominated by LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora}, which injects trainable low-rank matrices in a \emph{parallel} topology to ensure zero additional inference latency. 
Building upon this structural foundation, numerous studies have sought to optimize the training dynamics and representational capacity of parallel adapters. 
DoRA~\citep{liu2024dora} decomposes the weight updates into magnitude and direction components to stabilize fine-tuning. 
LoRA+~\citep{hayou2024lora+} addresses the gradient imbalance between adapter matrices by assigning distinct learning rates for $A$ and $B$. 
From a capacity allocation perspective, AdaLoRA~\citep{zhang2023adalora} dynamically distributes rank budgets based on singular value importance scores, while DyLoRA~\citep{valipour2022dylora} trains across multiple ranks simultaneously to avoid exhaustive search. 
Despite these sophisticated optimization strategies, they universally constrain the adapter to a \emph{static} parallel topology from initialization, inherently bypassing the pretrained manifold during the crucial early optimization steps.

\paragraph{The SVD Initialization Bottleneck.}
To mitigate the slow initial convergence caused by LoRA's random initialization, recent approaches have explored data-free matrix decompositions. 
Most notably, PiSSA~\citep{meng2024pissa} initializes the parallel adapters using the principal singular components of the frozen base weights ($\Wbase$). 
While this approach effectively leverages the architectural skeleton of the model to accelerate convergence, it introduces a severe computational bottleneck: applying full SVD to every massive target weight matrix incurs a prohibitive $O(d^3)$ cost. 
Furthermore, because the decomposition is performed statically before observing any data, it remains fundamentally \emph{task-agnostic}. 
\portal{} directly circumvents this dilemma. By delaying the decomposition to an optimal switching point $\Tswitch$, \portal{} requires only a negligible $O(dr^2)$ double SVD on a dynamically learned, \emph{task-aware} matrix, eliminating the initialization bottleneck entirely.

\paragraph{Sequential Topologies and Time-Axis Generalization.}
Historically, early PEFT methods such as Houlsby adapters~\citep{houlsby2019parameter} employed a \emph{sequential} (series) topology, inserting bottleneck layers directly after the pretrained layers. 
This structure possesses a profound mathematical advantage: it utilizes the feature space of $\Wbase$ as an implicit prior, acting as a projection lens to efficiently extract task-relevant signals. AdapterFusion~\citep{pfeiffer2020adapterfusion} further explored combining multiple such adapters via attention mechanisms. 
However, sequential adapters incur inference latency strictly proportional to the model's depth, leading to their gradual abandonment in favor of parallel methods. 
\portal{} uniquely resolves this historical dichotomy. By generalizing the topology over the training time axis via a mathematically lossless \emph{Task-Isomorphic Projection}, \portal{} harvests the rapid, manifold-aligned representation power of sequential adapters while ultimately culminating in the zero-latency structure of parallel adapters.

\paragraph{Intrinsic Dimensionality and Broad PEFT Paradigms.}
The theoretical justification for low-rank approaches is deeply rooted in the intrinsic dimensionality perspective~\citep{aghajanyan2021intrinsic}, which posits that over-parameterized models can be effectively fine-tuned within a highly restricted, low-dimensional subspace. 
Within this broader ecosystem, BitFit~\citep{zaken2022bitfit} isolates optimization to only bias terms, Delta-LoRA~\citep{zi2023delta} explores propagating adapter deltas back to the base weights, and QLoRA~\citep{dettmers2023qlora} enables LoRA on heavily quantized models to maximize memory efficiency. 
\portal{} seamlessly aligns with these paradigms; its activation-based probing and lightweight projection make it inherently compatible with quantized base models, pushing the boundaries of both computational efficiency and optimization dynamics.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Preliminaries: Parallel vs. Sequential Adapters}

Consider a pretrained linear layer $\Wbase \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$ and a low-rank adapter pair parameterized by $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$, where the rank $r \ll \min(d_{\mathrm{out}}, d_{\mathrm{in}})$. 
Historically, PEFT architectures bifurcate into two canonical topologies based on the adapter's input injection point:

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Comparison of fundamental adapter topologies. \portal{} bridges this historical dichotomy by starting in sequential mode and transitioning to parallel via a Task-Isomorphic Projection.}
\label{tab:topology}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
& \textbf{Parallel (LoRA)} & \textbf{Sequential (Houlsby)} \\
\midrule
Forward Pass          & $h = \Wbase x + BAx$           & $h = \Wbase x + BA(\Wbase x)$ \\
Adapter Input         & $x$ (raw input)             & $\Wbase x$ (pretrained output) \\
Effective $\Delta W$  & $BA$                   & $BA\Wbase$ \\
Inference Latency     & None (mergeable)        & $O(dr)$ per layer \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

While the parallel topology is favored for its zero-latency inference, the sequential topology possesses a profound, often overlooked optimization advantage. 
In sequential mode, the gradient with respect to $A$ is formulated as $\nabla_A \mathcal{L} = B^\top (\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial h)(\Wbase x)^\top$. 
Because the update operates directly on the pretrained feature space $\Wbase x$, it leverages an implicit \emph{spectral prior}. This strongly biases the early optimization steps toward the principal directions where $\Wbase$ already holds robust representations, acting as a natural, data-driven curriculum. 
In contrast, LoRA's gradient $\nabla_A \mathcal{L} = B^\top (\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial h) x^\top$ treats all input directions uniformly, often leading to inefficient early exploration in a suboptimal subspace.

\subsection{\portal{}: Time-Axis Generalization}
\label{sec:portal}

To harness the representational power of sequential probing without sacrificing parallel inference efficiency, \portal{} generalizes adapter connectivity as a continuous function of training progress. 
Let $t \in [0, 1]$ denote the normalized training epoch. The adapter operates in a sequential mode for $t < \Tswitch$ and strictly transitions to a parallel mode for $t \geq \Tswitch$:

\begin{equation}
h(t) = \Wbase x + B(t) A(t) \cdot
\begin{cases}
\Wbase x & \text{if } t < \Tswitch \quad (\text{Sequential Phase})\\
x & \text{if } t \geq \Tswitch \quad (\text{Parallel Phase})
\end{cases}
\label{eq:portal}
\end{equation}

Within this unified framework, standard LoRA is mathematically recovered as the extreme special case where $\Tswitch = 0$, while $\Tswitch = 1$ yields a pure sequential adapter. 
Intermediate values of $\Tswitch$ enable a \emph{task-aware sequential warm-up} that effectively probes the optimal subspace before locking into the parallel space for fine-grained tuning.

\paragraph{Initialization Dynamics.}
During the initial sequential phase ($t < \Tswitch$), we initialize $A$ with Kaiming uniform and $B \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10^{-4})$. 
The near-zero initialization of $B$ ensures that $BA(\Wbase x) \approx 0$, preserving the pretrained output at the start of training. Simultaneously, the variance in $A$ ensures it immediately receives rich, meaningful gradient signals projected through the $\Wbase$ manifold.

\subsection{Task-Isomorphic Projection via Rank-Bounded SVD}
\label{sec:projection}

At the transition point $t = \Tswitch$, the learned sequential adapter $(\Bseq, \Aseq)$ must be converted into an equivalent parallel adapter $(\Bpar, \Apar)$. To ensure the functional output of the network is perfectly preserved for any input $x$, we require an algebraic equivalence:
\begin{equation}
\Bpar \Apar x = \Bseq \Aseq (\Wbase x) \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Bpar \Apar = \Bseq \Aseq \Wbase \triangleq M
\label{eq:equivalence}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{The Rank Bound of $M$.}
The target projection matrix $M \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$ encapsulates all task-adapted knowledge acquired during the sequential phase. Crucially, because $M$ is formed by the inner product with the low-rank matrices $\Bseq \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times r}$ and $\Aseq \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$, its rank is strictly bounded: 
\begin{equation*}
    \mathrm{rank}(M) \leq r
\end{equation*}

We exploit this fundamental mathematical property to completely bypass the intractable $O(d^3)$ full-weight SVD required by prior methods like PiSSA.
\paragraph{The Projection Pipeline.} 
Instead of a full decomposition, we factorize $M$ into $\Bpar$ and $\Apar$ through an ultra-fast, $\mathbf{O(dr^2)}$ sequential pipeline:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Step 1: Subspace Extraction (Double QR).} We extract the orthogonal bases $Q_B \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times r}$ and $Q_X \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{in}} \times r}$ by performing thin QR decompositions directly on the lightweight sequential components:
    \begin{equation}
    \Bseq = Q_B R_B, \quad (\Aseq \Wbase)^\top = Q_X R_X
    \end{equation}
    
    \item \textbf{Step 2: Core Rank-$r$ SVD and Reconstruction.} The problem is now reduced to decomposing the microscopic $r \times r$ core matrix, $R_B R_X^\top$. By performing SVD strictly on this core, we identically reconstruct the full singular vectors ($U$ and $V$) of $M$:
    \begin{align}
    R_B R_X^\top &= \widetilde{U} \Sigma \widetilde{V}^\top \quad \text{(Core SVD)} \\
    M &= \underbrace{(Q_B \widetilde{U})}_{U} \Sigma \underbrace{(\widetilde{V}^\top Q_X^\top)}_{V^\top} \quad \text{(Reconstruction)}
    \end{align}
    
    \item \textbf{Step 3: Symmetric Splitting.} To factorize the reconstructed matrix $M = U \Sigma V^\top$ into the parallel adapters, we enforce a symmetric energy distribution across the singular values:
    \begin{equation}
    \Bpar = U \Sigma^{1/2}, \quad \Apar = \Sigma^{1/2} V^\top
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
The necessity of the symmetric splitting in Step 3 is governed by the following optimization principle:

\begin{theorem}[Optimal Conditioning]
\label{thm:condition}
Among all possible factorizations $M = B'A'$ of the form $B' = U\Sigma^\alpha$ and $A' = \Sigma^{1-\alpha} V^\top$ for $\alpha \in [0,1]$, the symmetric split ($\alpha = 1/2$) strictly minimizes the maximum condition number: $\max(\kappa(\Bpar), \kappa(\Apar)) = \sqrt{\kappa(M)}$.
\end{theorem}

This balanced distribution effectively prevents the gradient vanishing or exploding phenomena that commonly occur under asymmetric configurations (see Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} for proof).

\paragraph{Continuous Transition Dynamics.}
To ensure the optimization process remains perfectly stable after the structural shift at $\Tswitch$, we address two critical transition dynamics:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Algebraic Continuity:} As guaranteed by Eq.~\ref{eq:equivalence}, $h(t = \Tswitch^-) = h(t = \Tswitch^+)$. Consequently, the loss trajectory remains continuous ($\mathcal{L}(\Tswitch^-) = \mathcal{L}(\Tswitch^+)$), completely averting catastrophic loss spikes during the switch.
    \item \textbf{Stale Inertia Reset:} The optimizer's momentum and variance accumulators encode curvature information specific to the sequential parameter space, which is invalid in the parallel space. We explicitly reset these states to zero at $\Tswitch$, akin to a warm restart~\citep{loshchilov2019adamw}, preventing stale inertia from corrupting the parallel trajectory.
\end{enumerate}


\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\portal{}: Time-Axis Generalized Training via Task-Isomorphic Projection}
\label{alg:portal}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Pretrained weight $\Wbase$, adapter rank $r$, switching step $\Tswitch$, total steps $T$
\STATE Initialize $A \sim$ Kaiming Uniform, $B \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10^{-4})$
\STATE Set adapter topology $\gets$ \texttt{Sequential}
\FOR{step $t = 0$ \TO $T$}
    \IF{$t = \Tswitch$}
        \STATE $M \gets B \cdot A \cdot \Wbase$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Construct effective task-adapted matrix
        \STATE $U, \Sigma, V^\top \gets \textsc{RankR-DoubleSVD}(M)$ \hfill $\triangleright$ $O(dr^2)$ lossless extraction via Steps 1-2
        \STATE $B \gets U\Sigma^{1/2}$, $\; A \gets \Sigma^{1/2} V^\top$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Theorem~\ref{thm:condition}: Symmetric splitting
        \STATE Reset optimizer momentum and variance to $\mathbf{0}$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Clear stale inertia
        \STATE Set adapter topology $\gets$ \texttt{Parallel}
    \ENDIF
    \STATE Forward: $h \gets \Wbase x + BA \cdot (\Wbase x \text{ if Sequential, else } x)$
    \STATE Compute Loss $\mathcal{L}$, Backward pass, and Update $(B, A)$
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} $\Wbase + BA$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Merge weights for zero-latency inference
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%=============================================================================

To empirically validate the \portal{} framework, we design a comprehensive evaluation suite spanning natural language understanding (NLU), complex reasoning, and generation. Our primary objective is to demonstrate that \portal{} not only matches or exceeds the performance of state-of-the-art PEFT methods but also provides critical geometric insights into task-manifold alignment via its transitioning dynamics.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Models and Datasets.}
We evaluate across two distinct model scales and architectures: 
(1)~\textbf{DeBERTa-v3-base}~\citep{he2023debertav3} (86M) for NLU capabilities, tested on seven diverse GLUE~\citep{wang2019glue} benchmarks.
(2)~\textbf{LLaMA-2-7B}~\citep{touvron2023llama2} (7B) for advanced Natural Language Generation (NLG). We fine-tune and evaluate on three challenging domains: mathematical reasoning (MetaMathQA~\citep{yu2024metamath} $\to$ GSM8K~\citep{cobbe2021gsm8k} and MATH~\citep{hendrycks2021math}), code generation (CodeFeedback~\citep{zhuo2024codefeedback} $\to$ HumanEval~\citep{chen2021humaneval} and MBPP~\citep{austin2021mbpp}), and multi-turn open-ended conversation (WizardLM~\citep{xu2024wizardlm} $\to$ MT-Bench~\citep{zheng2023mtbench}).

\paragraph{Baselines and Implementation Details.}
We benchmark \portal{} against the canonical standard LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora}, the recent SVD-based SOTA PiSSA~\citep{meng2024pissa}, the direction-magnitude decoupled DoRA~\citep{liu2024dora}, and the bias-only BitFit~\citep{zaken2022bitfit}. 
To ensure a rigorous and fair comparison, all low-rank methods are strictly constrained to rank $r = 8$ and applied to identical target modules. For DeBERTa, adapters target the query, key, and value projections. For LLaMA-2-7B, we inject adapters into all seven linear projections ($W_q, W_k, W_v, W_o, W_{\mathrm{gate}}, W_{\mathrm{up}}, W_{\mathrm{down}}$), capping the trainable parameters at a highly efficient $0.30\%$. 
Models are fine-tuned for 1 epoch on 100K subsampled training examples. For \portal{}, we report the performance using the optimal switching point $\Tswitch$ per task category (comprehensive $\Tswitch$ ablations are detailed in Section~\ref{sec:ablation}).

\textbf{The Geometry of Mathematical Reasoning:} The divergence in performance between GSM8K and MATH provides a profound insight into task-manifold alignment. GSM8K primarily requires rigid formatting and step-by-step arithmetic memorization. Consequently, methods that jump directly into parallel orthogonal spaces (LoRA) or enforce full-weight static initialization (PiSSA) perform well by forcefully overwriting representations. Conversely, the MATH dataset demands deep, multi-step symbolic logic. By utilizing a manifold-aware sequential warm-up ($\Tswitch=0.8$), \portal{} perfectly preserves the base model's intrinsic logical structures rather than destroying them, resulting in the SOTA MATH performance.

\textbf{Efficiency vs. Performance Trade-off in Code Generation:} For code generation (HumanEval, MBPP), PiSSA's static $O(d^3)$ SVD initialization provides a marginal edge in pass@1 metrics. However, \portal{} remains highly competitive, outperforming standard LoRA while fundamentally bypassing the immense computational initialization bottleneck of PiSSA. \portal{} achieves this near-parity through its dynamic, data-driven probing and ultra-lightweight $O(dr^2)$ projection, demonstrating a vastly superior trade-off between initialization overhead and final generative capability.

\label{sec:alignment}

The optimal $\Tswitch$ varies systematically across tasks, reflecting the alignment between the task and $\Wbase$'s manifold:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High alignment} (NLU classification): $\Tswitch \to 1.0$. The pretrained features are directly useful; extended sequential exploitation is optimal.
    \item \textbf{Moderate alignment} (code, conversation): $\Tswitch \approx 0.5$. The task builds upon pretrained knowledge but requires significant new directions.
    \item \textbf{Low alignment} (novel reasoning): $\Tswitch \to 0.01$. A brief micro-probe extracts an initial directional signal before releasing the adapter to explore freely in parallel mode.
\end{itemize}

This positions $\Tswitch$ as a principled dial between \emph{knowledge retention} (high $\Tswitch$, exploiting what the model knows) and \emph{knowledge acquisition} (low $\Tswitch$, learning new representations).

\subsection{Optimization dynamics at the switching boundary}

The Task-Isomorphic Projection ensures a smooth transition:
(1)~By Proposition~\ref{prop:lossless}, the forward output is exactly preserved, so $\mathcal{L}(\Tswitch^-) = \mathcal{L}(\Tswitch^+)$ with no loss spike.
(2)~The momentum reset prevents stale sequential-space curvature from corrupting parallel-phase updates.
(3)~Symmetric splitting (Theorem~\ref{thm:condition}) equalizes gradient magnitudes, enabling both $\Bpar$ and $\Apar$ to receive informative gradients from the first post-switch step.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

We introduced \portal{}, a framework that generalizes PEFT over the training time axis by dynamically transitioning adapter connectivity from sequential to parallel mode.
The Task-Isomorphic Projection losslessly converts sequential adapters into parallel adapters via rank-$r$ double SVD with $O(dr^2)$ cost.
Experiments demonstrate that \portal{} consistently matches or outperforms LoRA, PiSSA, and DoRA across NLU, mathematical reasoning, code generation, and conversation.
The $\Tswitch$ spectrum provides a principled mechanism for balancing knowledge retention and acquisition, with the optimal value reflecting the intrinsic alignment between task and pretrained manifold.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The optimal $\Tswitch$ requires validation-based selection; an adaptive mechanism based on gradient coherence remains future work.
Our experiments cover models up to 7B parameters; scaling behavior on larger models warrants investigation.

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
\portal{} reduces the computational barrier to high-quality LLM fine-tuning while maintaining the same parameter and inference budget as LoRA, making model adaptation more accessible.

\section{Proofs}
\label{app:proofs}

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:condition}}
\begin{proof}
Let $M = U\Sigma V^\top$ with $\Sigma = \diag(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r)$, $\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$.
For a factorization $B' = U\Sigma^\alpha$, $A' = \Sigma^{1-\alpha} V^\top$ with $\alpha \in [0,1]$:
\begin{align}
    \kappa(B') = \kappa(M)^\alpha, \quad \kappa(A') = \kappa(M)^{1-\alpha}.
\end{align}
The minimax problem $\min_\alpha \max(\kappa(B'), \kappa(A')) = \min_\alpha \kappa(M)^{\max(\alpha, 1-\alpha)}$ is solved at $\alpha = 1/2$, yielding $\kappa(B') = \kappa(A') = \sqrt{\kappa(M)}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Gradient flow analysis}

Let $\Wbase = U_W \Sigma_W V_W^\top$.
The sequential gradient $\nabla_A \mathcal{L}|_{\mathrm{seq}} \propto (\Wbase x)^\top = (V_W \Sigma_W U_W^\top x)^\top$ amplifies input components aligned with $\Wbase$'s right singular vectors, weighted by their singular values.
This provides a natural curriculum: the adapter first adapts along directions where $\Wbase$ has the strongest signal.
The parallel gradient $\nabla_A \mathcal{L}|_{\mathrm{par}} \propto x^\top$ lacks this spectral bias, treating all directions equally.

\section{Additional ablation studies}
\label{app:ablations}

\subsection{Asymmetric vs.\ symmetric splitting}

An asymmetric split ($B' = U\Sigma$, $A' = V^\top$) preserves the forward output but creates a conditioning imbalance: $\kappa(B') = \kappa(M)$ while $\kappa(A') = 1$.
In our MetaMath experiments, asymmetric splitting caused gradient norm spikes of 2--5$\times$ in the first 100 post-switch steps, degrading MATH accuracy by 0.3--0.5 points compared to symmetric splitting.
