\begin{abstract}
%=============================================================================
Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods such as LoRA fix the adapter topology to a \emph{parallel} connection, bypassing the pretrained weight manifold ($\Wbase$) that sequential adapters exploit for task adaptation.
SVD-based initialization methods (e.g., PiSSA) attempt to utilize $\Wbase$ but require $O(d^3)$ decomposition and remain task-agnostic.
We introduce \portal{}, a PEFT framework that unifies sequential and parallel adaptations along the training time axis.
During an initial sequential phase, \portal{} projects activations through $\Wbase$ ($\Wbase x$) to identify the task-intrinsic subspace from data.
At a switching step $\Tswitch$, a lossless \emph{Task-Isomorphic Projection} converts the learned sequential representations into an equivalent parallel adapter via a lightweight double SVD.
This costs $O(dr^2)$---orders of magnitude below full-weight SVD---while preserving the zero additional latency property of LoRA at inference.
Standard LoRA is recovered as the special case $\Tswitch = 0$.
Experiments on LLaMA-2-7B and DeBERTa-v3-base across mathematical reasoning (GSM8K, MATH), code generation (HumanEval, MBPP), conversation (MT-Bench), and seven GLUE tasks show that \portal{} consistently outperforms LoRA, PiSSA, and DoRA, achieving the best score on 6 out of 7 NLU tasks and the highest MATH accuracy.
We also show that $\Tswitch$ serves as a geometric indicator of the alignment between the target task and the pretrained manifold.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

Large language models (LLMs) with billions of parameters~\citep{brown2020gpt3, touvron2023llama2} have become the \emph{de facto} foundation for natural language processing and reasoning tasks.
Full fine-tuning of these models is expensive in compute and memory, motivating parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods that update only a small fraction of parameters while keeping the pretrained weights frozen~\citep{lialin2023scaling}.

Among PEFT methods, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)~\citep{hu2022lora} has emerged as the dominant paradigm.
LoRA injects trainable low-rank matrices $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}$ in \emph{parallel} with each frozen weight $\Wbase$, yielding the forward pass $h = \Wbase x + BAx$.\footnote{For notational simplicity in the introduction, we assume $d_{\mathrm{in}} = d_{\mathrm{out}} = d$. The general case is treated in Section~\ref{sec:method}.}
The adapter can be reparameterized into $\Wbase$ after training, guaranteeing \emph{zero additional inference latency}.
However, LoRA's random initialization means the early optimization steps operate in a subspace disconnected from the pretrained representations of $\Wbase$, leading to slow initial convergence.

To mitigate this, PiSSA~\citep{meng2024pissa} initializes $B$ and $A$ using the principal singular vectors of $\Wbase$.
While SVD-based initialization accelerates convergence, it requires a full SVD over every target weight matrix at $O(d^3)$ cost per layer.
Because this decomposition is performed before observing any data, it captures the structure of $\Wbase$ but remains \emph{task-agnostic}.

The earliest PEFT methods employed a \emph{sequential} topology~\citep{houlsby2019parameter}, where the adapter processes the output of the base layer: $h = \Wbase x + BA(\Wbase x)$.
This structure uses the feature space of $\Wbase$ as an implicit prior, projecting activations through the pretrained manifold before adaptation.
Sequential adapters were abandoned in favor of parallel methods because they incur inference latency proportional to the model's depth.

We challenge the assumption that sequential and parallel topologies must be mutually exclusive.
We introduce \portal{} (\textbf{P}arameter-efficient \textbf{O}ptimization via \textbf{R}ank-bounded \textbf{T}ask-isomorphic \textbf{A}dapter \textbf{L}ifting), a framework that \emph{generalizes} adapter connectivity over the training time axis.
\portal{} combines the optimization benefits of sequential probing with the inference efficiency of parallel adaptation. Our contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item[\bf C1.] \textbf{Time-Axis Generalized PEFT Framework:} We formulate a novel paradigm where the adapter topology dynamically transitions from sequential to parallel at a configurable switching point $\Tswitch \in [0, 1]$. Standard LoRA is mathematically unified within this framework as the extreme special case where $\Tswitch = 0$.
    \item[\bf C2.] \textbf{Task-Aware Manifold Probing:} The initial sequential phase acts as a data-driven probe of the pretrained manifold, identifying the task-intrinsic subspace and achieving faster convergence than random (LoRA) or static (PiSSA) initializations.
    \item[\bf C3.] \textbf{Fast SVD Initialization:} The \emph{Task-Isomorphic Projection} converts the learned sequential representations into an equivalent parallel adapter via a rank-$r$ double SVD at $O(dr^2)$ cost, eliminating PiSSA's $O(d^3)$ initialization bottleneck without loss of representation power.
    \item[\bf C4.] \textbf{Zero Inference Latency:} After projection at $\Tswitch$, training continues in a purely parallel state, structurally identical to standard LoRA, preserving zero additional inference latency.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{Parallel Low-Rank Adaptation and Optimization.}
LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} injects trainable low-rank matrices in a \emph{parallel} topology to ensure zero additional inference latency.
Subsequent work optimizes training dynamics and representational capacity:
DoRA~\citep{liu2024dora} decomposes weight updates into magnitude and direction components;
LoRA+~\citep{hayou2024lora+} assigns distinct learning rates for $A$ and $B$ to address gradient imbalance;
AdaLoRA~\citep{zhang2023adalora} distributes rank budgets based on singular value importance; and
DyLoRA~\citep{valipour2022dylora} trains across multiple ranks simultaneously.
All of these constrain the adapter to a \emph{static} parallel topology, bypassing the pretrained manifold during early optimization.

\paragraph{The SVD Initialization Bottleneck.}
PiSSA~\citep{meng2024pissa} initializes parallel adapters using the principal singular components of $\Wbase$, accelerating convergence but requiring $O(d^3)$ full SVD per layer.
Because this decomposition occurs before observing any data, it remains \emph{task-agnostic}.
\portal{} delays decomposition to the switching point $\Tswitch$, requiring only an $O(dr^2)$ double SVD on a learned, \emph{task-aware} matrix.

\paragraph{Sequential Topologies and Time-Axis Generalization.}
Houlsby adapters~\citep{houlsby2019parameter} use a \emph{sequential} topology, inserting bottleneck layers after pretrained layers so the adapter operates on $\Wbase x$. AdapterFusion~\citep{pfeiffer2020adapterfusion} combines multiple such adapters via attention.
Sequential adapters incur inference latency proportional to model depth, which led to their abandonment.
\portal{} resolves this by generalizing the topology over the training time axis: a lossless \emph{Task-Isomorphic Projection} transfers the sequential adapter's manifold-aligned representations into the zero-latency parallel structure.

\paragraph{Intrinsic Dimensionality and Broad PEFT Paradigms.}
The intrinsic dimensionality perspective~\citep{aghajanyan2021intrinsic} shows that over-parameterized models can be fine-tuned within a low-dimensional subspace.
BitFit~\citep{zaken2022bitfit} optimizes only bias terms, Delta-LoRA~\citep{zi2023delta} propagates adapter deltas back to the base weights, and QLoRA~\citep{dettmers2023qlora} enables LoRA on quantized models.
\portal{} is compatible with these paradigms; its activation-based probing and lightweight projection work directly with quantized base models.

\subsection{Preliminaries: Parallel vs. Sequential Adapters}

Consider a pretrained linear layer $\Wbase \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$ and a low-rank adapter pair parameterized by $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$, where the rank $r \ll \min(d_{\mathrm{out}}, d_{\mathrm{in}})$. 
PEFT architectures split into two topologies based on the adapter's input injection point:

Let $\delta \triangleq \partial \mathcal{L}/\partial h$ denote the upstream gradient.
As shown in Table~\ref{tab:topology}, the sequential gradient $\nabla_A \mathcal{L} = B^\top \delta\, (\Wbase x)^\top$ operates on the pretrained feature space $\Wbase x$, biasing early optimization toward the principal directions of $\Wbase$.
In contrast, both LoRA and PiSSA compute $\nabla_A \mathcal{L} = B^\top \delta\, x^\top$, treating all input directions uniformly regardless of pretrained structure.
PiSSA partially addresses this via SVD-based initialization but incurs $O(d^3)$ cost per layer and remains task-agnostic.
This motivates \portal{}'s approach: combining sequential probing with parallel inference, detailed in the following sections.

\subsection{\portal{}: Time-Axis Generalization}
\label{sec:portal}

\portal{} generalizes adapter connectivity as a function of training progress, combining sequential probing with parallel inference efficiency.
Let $t \in [0, 1]$ denote the normalized training epoch. The adapter operates in a sequential mode for $t < \Tswitch$ and strictly transitions to a parallel mode for $t \geq \Tswitch$:

\begin{equation}
h(t) = \Wbase x + B(t) A(t) \cdot
\begin{cases}
\Wbase x & \text{if } t < \Tswitch \quad (\text{Sequential Phase})\\
x & \text{if } t \geq \Tswitch \quad (\text{Parallel Phase})
\end{cases}
\label{eq:portal}
\end{equation}

Standard LoRA is recovered as $\Tswitch = 0$; $\Tswitch = 1$ yields a pure sequential adapter.
Intermediate values enable a task-aware sequential warm-up that probes the subspace before switching to parallel mode.
For brevity, we denote the weights during the sequential phase ($t < \Tswitch$) as $\Bseq, \Aseq$, and the parallel phase ($t \ge \Tswitch$) as $\Bpar, \Apar$.


\subsection{Task-Isomorphic Projection via Rank-Bounded SVD}
\label{sec:projection}

At the transition point $t = \Tswitch$, the learned sequential adapter $(\Bseq, \Aseq)$ must be converted into an equivalent parallel adapter $(\Bpar, \Apar)$. To ensure the functional output of the network is perfectly preserved for any input $x$, we require an algebraic equivalence:
\begin{equation}
\Bpar \Apar x = \Bseq \Aseq (\Wbase x) \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Bpar \Apar = \Bseq \Aseq \Wbase \triangleq M
\label{eq:equivalence}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{The Rank Bound of $M$.}
The target projection matrix $M \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$ contains all task-adapted knowledge from the sequential phase. Because $M = \Bseq \Aseq \Wbase$ with $\Bseq \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times r}$ and $\Aseq \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$, its rank is bounded:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathrm{rank}(M) \leq r
\end{equation*}

This rank bound lets us bypass the $O(d^3)$ full-weight SVD.
\paragraph{The Projection Pipeline.} 
Instead of a full decomposition, we factorize $M$ into $\Bpar$ and $\Apar$ through an $\mathbf{O(dr^2)}$ pipeline:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Step 1: Subspace Extraction (Double QR).} We extract the orthogonal bases $Q_B \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times r}$ and $Q_X \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{in}} \times r}$ by performing thin QR decompositions directly on the lightweight sequential components:
    \begin{equation}
    \Bseq = Q_B R_B, \quad (\Aseq \Wbase)^\top = Q_X R_X
    \end{equation}
    
    \item \textbf{Step 2: Core Rank-$r$ SVD and Reconstruction.} The problem is now reduced to decomposing the microscopic $r \times r$ core matrix, $R_B R_X^\top$. By performing SVD strictly on this core, we identically reconstruct the full singular vectors ($U$ and $V$) of $M$:
    \begin{align}
    R_B R_X^\top &= \widetilde{U} \Sigma \widetilde{V}^\top \quad \text{(Core SVD)} \\
    M &= \underbrace{(Q_B \widetilde{U})}_{U} \Sigma \underbrace{(\widetilde{V}^\top Q_X^\top)}_{V^\top} \quad \text{(Reconstruction)}
    \end{align}
    
    \item \textbf{Step 3: Symmetric Splitting.} To factorize the reconstructed matrix $M = U \Sigma V^\top$ into the parallel adapters, we enforce a symmetric energy distribution across the singular values:
    \begin{equation}
    \Bpar = U \Sigma^{1/2}, \quad \Apar = \Sigma^{1/2} V^\top
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Optimization Stability via Symmetric Splitting.}
The energy distribution in Step 3 is a critical design choice for post-switch convergence. A naive or asymmetric factorization (e.g., assigning all singular values to one matrix) creates a scale imbalance between $\Bpar$ and $\Apar$, leading to exploding or vanishing gradients during backpropagation. We mitigate this through the principle of optimal conditioning:

\begin{theorem}[Optimal Conditioning]
\label{thm:condition}
Given the factorization $M = U \Sigma V^\top$, consider all rank-$r$ decompositions $M = B'A'$ where $B' = U\Sigma^\alpha$ and $A' = \Sigma^{1-\alpha} V^\top$ for $\alpha \in [0,1]$. The symmetric split ($\alpha = 1/2$) is the unique solution that minimizes the maximum condition number:
\begin{equation*}
    \min_{\alpha \in [0,1]} \max \left( \kappa(B'), \kappa(A') \right) = \sqrt{\kappa(M)}
\end{equation*}
\end{theorem}

By enforcing $\alpha=1/2$, \portal{} ensures that both adapter matrices reside in a numerically stable region of the loss landscape, preventing gradient imbalance immediately after the structural transition (proof in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}).

\paragraph{Seamless Optimization Handoff.}
To ensure the model passes through the structural boundary at $\Tswitch$ without optimization artifacts, we implement two stability mechanisms:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Loss Invariance:} Our Task-Isomorphic Projection (Eq.~\ref{eq:equivalence}) guarantees that the forward output remains strictly identical ($h_{seq} = h_{par}$). This ensures a zero-gap transition in the loss curve, preventing the "loss spikes" typically observed during architectural shifts.
    \item \textbf{Curvature Recalibration (Momentum Reset):} The optimizer's momentum ($m$) and variance ($v$) accumulators encode the curvature of the sequential manifold, which is geometrically incompatible with the newly projected parallel space. To prevent stale directional inertia from corrupting the new trajectory, we reset the optimizer states to zero at $\Tswitch$, initiating a clean-slate "warm restart"~\citep{loshchilov2019adamw} in the parallel subspace.
\end{itemize}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\portal{}: Time-Axis Generalized Training via Task-Isomorphic Projection}
\label{alg:portal}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Pretrained weight $\Wbase \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{out} \times d_{in}}$, rank $r$, switch step $\Tswitch$, total steps $T$
\STATE Initialize $A_{seq} \sim \mathcal{U}_{\text{Kaiming}}, \quad B_{seq} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10^{-4})$
\STATE Initialize optimizer states $m \gets \mathbf{0}, \quad v \gets \mathbf{0}$
\FOR{$t = 1$ \TO $T$}
    \IF{$t = \Tswitch$}
        \STATE $M \gets B_{seq} A_{seq} \Wbase$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Construct $M$
        \STATE $U, \Sigma, V^\top \gets \textsc{SVD}_r(M)$ \hfill $\triangleright$ SVD projection
        \STATE $A_{par} \gets \Sigma^{1/2} V^\top, \quad B_{par} \gets U\Sigma^{1/2}$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Energy splitting
        \STATE $m \gets \mathbf{0}, \quad v \gets \mathbf{0}$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Momentum reset
    \ENDIF
    
    \IF{$t < \Tswitch$}
        \STATE $h \gets (\mathbf{I} + B_{seq} A_{seq}) \Wbase x$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Sequential forward
    \ELSE
        \STATE $h \gets (\Wbase + B_{par} A_{par}) x$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Parallel forward
    \ENDIF
    
    \STATE $\mathcal{L} \gets \text{Loss}(h, y)$
    \STATE $(A, B), m, v \gets \text{Step}(\nabla_{\{A,B\}} \mathcal{L}, m, v)$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Update 
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} $\Wbase + B_{par} A_{par}$ \hfill
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Computational Complexity Analysis}

Table~\ref{tab:topology} summarizes the projection cost. PiSSA requires full-weight SVD of $\Wbase \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ at $O(d^3)$ per layer; \portal{} operates on the learned core matrix $\in \mathbb{R}^{r \times r}$, reducing the cost to $O(dr^2)$.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%=============================================================================

We evaluate \portal{} on natural language understanding (NLU), reasoning, and generation tasks, comparing against state-of-the-art PEFT methods and analyzing task-manifold alignment via the $\Tswitch$ dynamics.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Models and Datasets.}
We evaluate on two model scales:
(1)~\textbf{DeBERTa-v3-base}~\citep{he2023debertav3} (86M) on seven GLUE~\citep{wang2019glue} benchmarks.
(2)~\textbf{LLaMA-2-7B}~\citep{touvron2023llama2} (7B) on mathematical reasoning (MetaMathQA~\citep{yu2024metamath} $\to$ GSM8K~\citep{cobbe2021gsm8k} and MATH~\citep{hendrycks2021math}), code generation (CodeFeedback~\citep{zhuo2024codefeedback} $\to$ HumanEval~\citep{chen2021humaneval} and MBPP~\citep{austin2021mbpp}), and conversation (WizardLM~\citep{xu2024wizardlm} $\to$ MT-Bench~\citep{zheng2023mtbench}).

\paragraph{Baselines and Implementation Details.}
Baselines: LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} (Gaussian and Kaiming variants), PiSSA~\citep{meng2024pissa}, DoRA~\citep{liu2024dora}, AdaLoRA~\citep{zhang2023adalora}, and full fine-tuning.
All low-rank methods use rank $r = 8$ on identical target modules. For DeBERTa, adapters target query, key, and value projections. For LLaMA-2-7B, adapters target all seven linear projections ($W_q, W_k, W_v, W_o, W_{\mathrm{gate}}, W_{\mathrm{up}}, W_{\mathrm{down}}$), totaling $0.30\%$ trainable parameters.
Models are fine-tuned for 1 epoch on 100K subsampled training examples. We report \portal{} with the optimal $\Tswitch$ per task category ($\Tswitch$ ablations in Section~\ref{sec:ablation}).

The Task-Isomorphic Projection ensures a stable transition at $\Tswitch$ through three mechanisms:

\begin{figure}[t]

\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/lossless_transition.pdf}
\caption{Training loss around $\Tswitch\!=\!0.2$ (MetaMath). \textbf{(a)}~Full curve with phase coloring. \textbf{(b)}~Zoom-in: step losses (dots) and smoothed trend (line) show no spike at the transition, confirming the lossless projection (Eq.~\ref{eq:equivalence}).}

Figure~\ref{fig:transition} confirms that the transition is smooth. Three mechanisms ensure this:

\paragraph{Output-preserving projection (no loss spike).}
By Eq.~\ref{eq:equivalence}, the forward output is preserved: $h(\Tswitch^-) = h(\Tswitch^+)$. Figure~\ref{fig:transition}(b) shows the loss passes through the boundary without discontinuity.

\paragraph{Momentum reset.}
The optimizer's momentum and variance accumulators encode curvature from the sequential space $(\Bseq, \Aseq)$, invalid after projection to $(\Bpar, \Apar)$. Resetting to zero at $\Tswitch$ prevents stale states from corrupting the parallel trajectory~\citep{loshchilov2019adamw}.

\paragraph{Symmetric conditioning (balanced gradients).}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:condition}, the symmetric split reduces the maximum condition number from $\kappa(M)$ to $\sqrt{\kappa(M)}$, ensuring both $\Bpar$ and $\Apar$ receive informative gradients from the first post-switch step.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

We introduced \portal{}, a framework that generalizes PEFT over the training time axis by dynamically transitioning adapter connectivity from sequential to parallel mode.
The Task-Isomorphic Projection losslessly converts sequential adapters into parallel adapters via rank-$r$ double SVD with $O(dr^2)$ cost.
Experiments demonstrate that \portal{} consistently matches or outperforms LoRA, PiSSA, and DoRA across NLU, mathematical reasoning, code generation, and conversation.
The $\Tswitch$ spectrum provides a principled mechanism for balancing knowledge retention and acquisition, with the optimal value reflecting the intrinsic alignment between task and pretrained manifold.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The optimal $\Tswitch$ requires validation-based selection; an adaptive mechanism based on gradient coherence remains future work.
Our experiments cover models up to 7B parameters; scaling behavior on larger models warrants investigation.

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
\portal{} reduces the computational barrier to high-quality LLM fine-tuning while maintaining the same parameter and inference budget as LoRA, making model adaptation more accessible.

\section{Proofs}
\label{app:proofs}

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:condition}}
\begin{proof}
Let $M = U\Sigma V^\top$ with $\Sigma = \diag(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r)$, $\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$.
Since $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal matrices from the SVD, they do not affect the condition number: $\kappa(UX) = \kappa(X)$ for any orthogonal $U$.
For a factorization $B' = U\Sigma^\alpha$, $A' = \Sigma^{1-\alpha} V^\top$ with $\alpha \in [0,1]$, the condition number is determined solely by the diagonal $\Sigma^\alpha$:
\begin{align}
    \kappa(B') = \kappa(\Sigma^\alpha) = \kappa(M)^\alpha, \quad \kappa(A') = \kappa(\Sigma^{1-\alpha}) = \kappa(M)^{1-\alpha}.
\end{align}
The minimax problem $\min_\alpha \max(\kappa(B'), \kappa(A')) = \min_\alpha \kappa(M)^{\max(\alpha, 1-\alpha)}$ is solved at $\alpha = 1/2$, yielding $\kappa(B') = \kappa(A') = \sqrt{\kappa(M)}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Gradient flow analysis}

Let $\Wbase = U_W \Sigma_W V_W^\top$.
The sequential gradient $\nabla_A \mathcal{L}|_{\mathrm{seq}} \propto (\Wbase x)^\top = x^\top V_W \Sigma_W U_W^\top$ amplifies input components aligned with $\Wbase$'s right singular vectors, weighted by their singular values.
The adapter first adapts along directions where $\Wbase$ has the strongest signal.
The parallel gradient $\nabla_A \mathcal{L}|_{\mathrm{par}} \propto x^\top$ lacks this spectral bias and treats all directions equally.

\section{Additional ablation studies}
\label{app:ablations}

\subsection{Asymmetric vs.\ symmetric splitting}

An asymmetric split ($B' = U\Sigma$, $A' = V^\top$) preserves the forward output but creates a conditioning imbalance: $\kappa(B') = \kappa(M)$ while $\kappa(A') = 1$.
In our MetaMath experiments, asymmetric splitting caused gradient norm spikes of 2--5$\times$ in the first 100 post-switch steps, degrading MATH accuracy by 0.3--0.5 points compared to symmetric splitting.
