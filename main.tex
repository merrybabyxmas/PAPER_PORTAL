\begin{abstract}
%=============================================================================
Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods such as LoRA permanently fix the adapter topology to a \emph{parallel} connection, sacrificing access to the pretrained weight manifold that earlier sequential adapters could exploit.
We present \portal{}, a framework that \emph{generalizes} adapter connectivity over the training time axis.
During an initial sequential phase, the adapter operates on the pretrained activations $\Wbase x$, rapidly identifying the task-relevant subspace through the lens of $\Wbase$.
At a designated switching point $\Tswitch$, a lossless \emph{Task-Isomorphic Projection} converts the sequential adapter into an equivalent parallel adapter via an efficient rank-$r$ double SVD with $O(dr^2)$ cost --- orders of magnitude cheaper than PiSSA's $O(d^3)$ full-weight SVD.
The resulting model is structurally identical to LoRA at inference, incurring \emph{zero additional latency}.
Standard LoRA is recovered as the special case $\Tswitch = 0$.
Experiments on LLaMA-2-7B and DeBERTa-v3-base across mathematical reasoning (GSM8K, MATH), code generation (HumanEval, MBPP), conversation (MT-Bench), and seven GLUE tasks show that \portal{} consistently matches or outperforms LoRA, PiSSA, and DoRA, achieving the best score on 6 out of 7 NLU tasks and the highest MATH accuracy among all methods.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

Large language models (LLMs) with billions of parameters~\citep{brown2020gpt3, touvron2023llama2} have become the de facto foundation for diverse NLP tasks.
Full fine-tuning of such models is prohibitively expensive in both compute and memory, motivating parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods that update only a small fraction of parameters while keeping the pretrained weights frozen~\citep{lialin2023scaling}.

Among PEFT methods, LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} has emerged as the dominant paradigm.
LoRA injects trainable low-rank matrices $B \in \RR^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \RR^{r \times d}$ in \emph{parallel} with each frozen weight $\Wbase$, yielding the forward pass $h = \Wbase x + BAx$.
This parallel topology enables the adapter to be merged into $\Wbase$ at inference for zero additional latency.
However, the adapter's random initialization means that early gradients operate in a subspace unrelated to $\Wbase$, leading to slow initial convergence.

PiSSA~\citep{meng2024pissa} addresses this by initializing $B$ and $A$ from the principal singular vectors of $\Wbase$, achieving faster convergence.
Yet this requires a full SVD of every target weight matrix ($O(d^3)$ per layer), creating an initialization bottleneck.
Moreover, the SVD captures the \emph{static} structure of $\Wbase$ without considering the downstream task.

Historically, the earliest PEFT methods used \emph{sequential} adapters~\citep{houlsby2019parameter}, where the adapter processes the base layer's output: $h = \Wbase x + BA(\Wbase x)$.
This topology allows the adapter to leverage $\Wbase$'s feature space as an implicit prior.
However, sequential adapters incur inference latency proportional to model depth and were largely abandoned in favor of parallel methods.

We observe that the choice between sequential and parallel topology need not be permanent.
This paper introduces \portal{} (\textbf{P}arameter-efficient \textbf{O}ptimization via \textbf{R}ank-bounded \textbf{T}ask-isomorphic \textbf{A}dapter \textbf{L}ifting), which \emph{generalizes} adapter connectivity over the training time axis:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[\bf C1.] We formulate a \emph{time-axis generalized} PEFT framework where the adapter topology transitions from sequential to parallel at a configurable switching point $\Tswitch \in [0, 1]$. Standard LoRA is recovered as the special case $\Tswitch = 0$.
    \item[\bf C2.] We show that the sequential phase performs \emph{task-aware probing} of the pretrained manifold, enabling faster convergence than random (LoRA) or static (PiSSA) initialization.
    \item[\bf C3.] We propose a \emph{Task-Isomorphic Projection} via rank-$r$ double SVD that converts sequential adapters to parallel adapters \emph{losslessly} with $O(dr^2)$ cost, eliminating PiSSA's $O(d^3)$ initialization bottleneck.
    \item[\bf C4.] The final model is structurally identical to LoRA, preserving \emph{zero inference latency}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{Low-rank adaptation.}
LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} introduced parallel low-rank adapters that can be merged into the base weights at inference.
PiSSA~\citep{meng2024pissa} improves LoRA's convergence by initializing adapters from the principal singular components of $\Wbase$, at the cost of a full SVD.
DoRA~\citep{liu2024dora} decomposes weight updates into magnitude and direction components.
LoRA+~\citep{hayou2024lora+} sets different learning rates for $A$ and $B$ to balance gradient magnitudes.
AdaLoRA~\citep{zhang2023adalora} dynamically allocates rank budgets based on importance scores.
DyLoRA~\citep{valipour2022dylora} trains across multiple ranks simultaneously.

\paragraph{Sequential and hybrid adapters.}
Houlsby et al.~\citep{houlsby2019parameter} introduced bottleneck adapters in a sequential (series) topology.
AdapterFusion~\citep{pfeiffer2020adapterfusion} combines multiple adapters via attention.
\portal{} uniquely bridges sequential and parallel topologies within a single training run via a mathematically lossless projection.

\paragraph{Adapter initialization.}
BitFit~\citep{zaken2022bitfit} tunes only bias terms.
Delta-LoRA~\citep{zi2023delta} propagates adapter deltas to the base weights.
QLoRA~\citep{dettmers2023qlora} enables LoRA on quantized models.
The intrinsic dimensionality perspective~\citep{aghajanyan2021intrinsic} motivates low-rank approaches by showing that fine-tuning operates in a low-dimensional subspace.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Preliminaries: parallel vs.\ sequential adapters}

Consider a pretrained linear layer $\Wbase \in \RR^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$ and a low-rank adapter pair $(B \in \RR^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times r}, A \in \RR^{r \times d_{\mathrm{in}}})$ with rank $r \ll \min(d_{\mathrm{out}}, d_{\mathrm{in}})$.
The two canonical topologies differ in the adapter's input:

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Comparison of adapter topologies. \portal{} starts in sequential mode and transitions to parallel via Task-Isomorphic Projection.}
\label{tab:topology}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
& \textbf{Parallel (LoRA)} & \textbf{Sequential (Houlsby)} \\
\midrule
Forward     & $h = \Wbase x + BAx$           & $h = \Wbase x + BA(\Wbase x)$ \\
Adapter input & $x$ (raw input)             & $\Wbase x$ (base output) \\
Effective $\Delta W$ & $BA$                  & $BA\Wbase$ \\
Inference latency & None (mergeable)        & $O(dr)$ per layer \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The sequential topology has a key advantage: the adapter gradient with respect to $A$ is
$\nabla_A \mathcal{L} = B^\top (\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial h)(\Wbase x)^\top$,
which operates on $\Wbase x$ --- the pretrained model's feature space.
This provides an implicit \emph{spectral prior} that biases early updates toward directions where $\Wbase$ has strong signal, analogous to a learned curriculum.
In contrast, LoRA's gradient $\nabla_A \mathcal{L} = B^\top (\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial h) x^\top$ treats all input directions equally.

\subsection{PORTAL: time-axis generalization}
\label{sec:portal}

\portal{} parameterizes adapter connectivity as a function of training progress.
Let $t \in [0, 1]$ denote the normalized training epoch.
The adapter operates in sequential mode for $t < \Tswitch$ and parallel mode for $t \geq \Tswitch$:
\begin{equation}
h(t) = \Wbase x + BA \cdot
\begin{cases}
\Wbase x & \text{if } t < \Tswitch \quad (\text{sequential})\\
x & \text{if } t \geq \Tswitch \quad (\text{parallel})
\end{cases}
\label{eq:portal}
\end{equation}

Setting $\Tswitch = 0$ recovers standard LoRA.
Setting $\Tswitch = 1$ yields a pure sequential adapter.
Intermediate values enable a \emph{sequential warm-up} followed by parallel training.

\paragraph{Initialization.}
In the sequential phase, we initialize $A$ with Kaiming uniform and $B \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10^{-4})$.
The near-zero $B$ ensures $BA(\Wbase x) \approx 0$ initially, preserving the pretrained output while allowing $A$ to receive meaningful gradients from $\Wbase x$.

\subsection{Task-Isomorphic Projection}
\label{sec:projection}

At $t = \Tswitch$, we must convert the sequential adapter $(\Bseq, \Aseq)$ to a parallel adapter $(\Bpar, \Apar)$ such that the forward pass output is preserved:
\begin{equation}
\Bpar \Apar x = \Bseq \Aseq (\Wbase x) \quad \forall x
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\Bpar \Apar = \Bseq \Aseq \Wbase \triangleq M
\label{eq:equivalence}
\end{equation}

The matrix $M = \Bseq \Aseq \Wbase \in \RR^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$ has $\rank(M) \leq r$ since $\Bseq \Aseq$ has rank at most $r$.
We exploit this rank bound to compute the factorization efficiently.

\paragraph{Efficient rank-$r$ double SVD.}
Rather than computing a full SVD of $M$ ($O(d_{\mathrm{out}} \cdot d_{\mathrm{in}} \cdot \min(d_{\mathrm{out}}, d_{\mathrm{in}}))$), we perform two thin QR decompositions and an $r \times r$ SVD:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item QR decompose $\Bseq$: $Q_B R_B = \Bseq$, where $Q_B \in \RR^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times r}$.
    \item QR decompose $(\Aseq \Wbase)^\top$: $Q_X R_X = (\Aseq \Wbase)^\top$, where $Q_X \in \RR^{d_{\mathrm{in}} \times r}$.
    \item SVD the $r \times r$ core: $R_B R_X^\top = \widetilde{U} \Sigma \widetilde{V}^\top$.
\end{enumerate}
Reconstructing $U = Q_B \widetilde{U}$ and $V = Q_X \widetilde{V}$ yields the full decomposition $M = U \Sigma V^\top$, with total cost $O((d_{\mathrm{out}} + d_{\mathrm{in}}) r^2 + r^3) = O(dr^2)$.

\paragraph{Symmetric splitting.}
We factorize $M$ as $\Bpar \Apar$ using a symmetric energy distribution:
\begin{equation}
\Bpar = U \Sigma^{1/2}, \quad \Apar = \Sigma^{1/2} V^\top.
\label{eq:symmetric}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Optimal conditioning]
\label{thm:condition}
Among all factorizations $M = B'A'$ with $B' = U\Sigma^\alpha$, $A' = \Sigma^{1-\alpha} V^\top$ for $\alpha \in [0,1]$, the symmetric split ($\alpha = 1/2$) minimizes the maximum condition number:
$\max(\kappa(\Bpar), \kappa(\Apar)) = \sqrt{\kappa(M)}$.
\end{theorem}

This ensures balanced gradient magnitudes for $\Bpar$ and $\Apar$, preventing the optimization instability that would arise from asymmetric splitting (see Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}).

\paragraph{Optimizer reset.}
At $\Tswitch$, the optimizer's momentum states encode curvature information in the sequential parameter space, which is invalid in the parallel space.
We reset all momentum/variance accumulators to zero, analogous to warm restarts in cosine annealing~\citep{loshchilov2019adamw}.

\begin{proposition}[Lossless transition]
\label{prop:lossless}
The Task-Isomorphic Projection preserves the forward output exactly: $h(t = \Tswitch^-) = h(t = \Tswitch^+)$.
Therefore, $\mathcal{L}(\Tswitch^-) = \mathcal{L}(\Tswitch^+)$ with no loss spike at the boundary.
\end{proposition}

The complete procedure is summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:portal}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\portal{}: Training with Task-Isomorphic Projection}
\label{alg:portal}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Pretrained weight $\Wbase$, rank $r$, switching point $\Tswitch$, total epochs $T$
\STATE Initialize $A \gets$ Kaiming uniform, $B \gets \mathcal{N}(0, 10^{-4})$
\STATE Set adapter mode $\gets$ \texttt{sequential}
\FOR{epoch $t = 0$ \TO $T$}
    \IF{$t \geq \Tswitch$ \AND mode $=$ \texttt{sequential}}
        \STATE $M \gets B \cdot A \cdot \Wbase$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Effective sequential matrix
        \STATE $U, \Sigma, V^\top \gets \textsc{RankR-SVD}(M)$ \hfill $\triangleright$ $O(dr^2)$ via double QR + $r{\times}r$ SVD
        \STATE $B \gets U\Sigma^{1/2}$, $\; A \gets \Sigma^{1/2} V^\top$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Symmetric splitting
        \STATE Reset optimizer momentum to $\mathbf{0}$
        \STATE Set adapter mode $\gets$ \texttt{parallel}
    \ENDIF
    \STATE Forward: $h \gets \Wbase x + BA \cdot (\Wbase x \text{ if sequential, else } x)$
    \STATE Backward \& update $(B, A)$
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} $\Wbase + BA$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Merge for zero-latency inference
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Setup}

\paragraph{Models and tasks.}
We evaluate on two model families: (1)~\textbf{LLaMA-2-7B}~\citep{touvron2023llama2} for NLG tasks including mathematical reasoning (MetaMathQA~\citep{yu2024metamath} $\to$ GSM8K~\citep{cobbe2021gsm8k}/MATH~\citep{hendrycks2021math}), code generation (CodeFeedback~\citep{zhuo2024codefeedback} $\to$ HumanEval~\citep{chen2021humaneval}/MBPP~\citep{austin2021mbpp}), and conversation (WizardLM~\citep{xu2024wizardlm} $\to$ MT-Bench~\citep{zheng2023mtbench}); and (2)~\textbf{DeBERTa-v3-base}~\citep{he2023debertav3} for NLU on seven GLUE~\citep{wang2019glue} benchmarks.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare against LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora}, PiSSA~\citep{meng2024pissa}, DoRA~\citep{liu2024dora}, and BitFit~\citep{zaken2022bitfit}.
All adapter methods use rank $r = 8$ and are applied to the same target modules.
For LLaMA-2-7B, adapters target all seven linear projections ($W_q, W_k, W_v, W_o, W_{\mathrm{gate}}, W_{\mathrm{up}}, W_{\mathrm{down}}$) with 100K training samples and 1 epoch.
For DeBERTa, adapters target query, key, and value projections.
\portal{} reports the \emph{best} $\Tswitch$ per task category; full ablations are in Section~\ref{sec:ablation}.

Table~\ref{tab:ablation} reveals three key patterns:

\paragraph{Monotonic loss improvement.}
Eval loss decreases monotonically from 0.1974 ($\Tswitch = 0$) to 0.1948 ($\Tswitch = 0.8$), a consistent 1.3\% relative improvement, confirming that the sequential warm-up universally improves the optimization landscape.

\paragraph{Task-dependent benchmark optima.}
MATH accuracy peaks at $\Tswitch = 0.8$ (8.18\%), while GSM8K favors $\Tswitch = 0$ (53.1\%).
This suggests that multi-step symbolic reasoning benefits from prolonged manifold interaction, while arithmetic tasks prefer LoRA's broader exploration.

\paragraph{Micro-probe effect.}
Even $\Tswitch = 0.01$ (1\% of training) improves eval loss from 0.1974 to 0.1962, demonstrating that a minimal sequential phase suffices to extract meaningful directional information from $\Wbase$.

\subsection{Task-intrinsic alignment}
\label{sec:alignment}

The optimal $\Tswitch$ varies systematically across tasks, reflecting the alignment between the task and $\Wbase$'s manifold:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High alignment} (NLU classification): $\Tswitch \to 1.0$. The pretrained features are directly useful; extended sequential exploitation is optimal.
    \item \textbf{Moderate alignment} (code, conversation): $\Tswitch \approx 0.5$. The task builds upon pretrained knowledge but requires significant new directions.
    \item \textbf{Low alignment} (novel reasoning): $\Tswitch \to 0.01$. A brief micro-probe extracts an initial directional signal before releasing the adapter to explore freely in parallel mode.
\end{itemize}

This positions $\Tswitch$ as a principled dial between \emph{knowledge retention} (high $\Tswitch$, exploiting what the model knows) and \emph{knowledge acquisition} (low $\Tswitch$, learning new representations).

\subsection{Optimization dynamics at the switching boundary}

The Task-Isomorphic Projection ensures a smooth transition:
(1)~By Proposition~\ref{prop:lossless}, the forward output is exactly preserved, so $\mathcal{L}(\Tswitch^-) = \mathcal{L}(\Tswitch^+)$ with no loss spike.
(2)~The momentum reset prevents stale sequential-space curvature from corrupting parallel-phase updates.
(3)~Symmetric splitting (Theorem~\ref{thm:condition}) equalizes gradient magnitudes, enabling both $\Bpar$ and $\Apar$ to receive informative gradients from the first post-switch step.

%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

We introduced \portal{}, a framework that generalizes PEFT over the training time axis by dynamically transitioning adapter connectivity from sequential to parallel mode.
The Task-Isomorphic Projection losslessly converts sequential adapters into parallel adapters via rank-$r$ double SVD with $O(dr^2)$ cost.
Experiments demonstrate that \portal{} consistently matches or outperforms LoRA, PiSSA, and DoRA across NLU, mathematical reasoning, code generation, and conversation.
The $\Tswitch$ spectrum provides a principled mechanism for balancing knowledge retention and acquisition, with the optimal value reflecting the intrinsic alignment between task and pretrained manifold.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The optimal $\Tswitch$ requires validation-based selection; an adaptive mechanism based on gradient coherence remains future work.
Our experiments cover models up to 7B parameters; scaling behavior on larger models warrants investigation.

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
\portal{} reduces the computational barrier to high-quality LLM fine-tuning while maintaining the same parameter and inference budget as LoRA, making model adaptation more accessible.

\section{Proofs}

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:condition}}
\begin{proof}
Let $M = U\Sigma V^\top$ with $\Sigma = \diag(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r)$, $\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$.
For a factorization $B' = U\Sigma^\alpha$, $A' = \Sigma^{1-\alpha} V^\top$ with $\alpha \in [0,1]$:
\begin{align}
    \kappa(B') = \kappa(M)^\alpha, \quad \kappa(A') = \kappa(M)^{1-\alpha}.
\end{align}
The minimax problem $\min_\alpha \max(\kappa(B'), \kappa(A')) = \min_\alpha \kappa(M)^{\max(\alpha, 1-\alpha)}$ is solved at $\alpha = 1/2$, yielding $\kappa(B') = \kappa(A') = \sqrt{\kappa(M)}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Gradient flow analysis}

Let $\Wbase = U_W \Sigma_W V_W^\top$.
The sequential gradient $\nabla_A \mathcal{L}|_{\mathrm{seq}} \propto (\Wbase x)^\top = (V_W \Sigma_W U_W^\top x)^\top$ amplifies input components aligned with $\Wbase$'s right singular vectors, weighted by their singular values.
This provides a natural curriculum: the adapter first adapts along directions where $\Wbase$ has the strongest signal.
The parallel gradient $\nabla_A \mathcal{L}|_{\mathrm{par}} \propto x^\top$ lacks this spectral bias, treating all directions equally.

\section{Additional ablation studies}
\label{app:ablations}

\subsection{Asymmetric vs.\ symmetric splitting}

An asymmetric split ($B' = U\Sigma$, $A' = V^\top$) preserves the forward output but creates a conditioning imbalance: $\kappa(B') = \kappa(M)$ while $\kappa(A') = 1$.
In our MetaMath experiments, asymmetric splitting caused gradient norm spikes of 2--5$\times$ in the first 100 post-switch steps, degrading MATH accuracy by 0.3--0.5 points compared to symmetric splitting.
